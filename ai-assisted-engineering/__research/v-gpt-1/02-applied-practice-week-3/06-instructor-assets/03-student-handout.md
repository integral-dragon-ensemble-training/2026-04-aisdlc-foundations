# Student Handout

## Main idea

AI-assisted engineering is most valuable when it helps teams see, reduce, and systematically pay down the friction and technical debt that make software hard to change.

## The five-part method

1. Define what good looks like.
2. Identify the debt that blocks it.
3. Use AI to analyze and improve it.
4. Validate improvements with tests, observability, and security.
5. Turn that into a repeatable engineering practice.

## What “good” looks like

A healthy software project is:

- documented enough to trust,
- architecturally understandable,
- reproducible to set up,
- meaningfully testable,
- buildable and releasable,
- disciplined about data and schema,
- observable in runtime,
- reasonable about security hygiene,
- and supported by workable team conventions.

## Technical debt, simplified

Technical debt is anything that increases the cost, time, or risk of future change.

It may appear as:
- stale docs,
- unclear boundaries,
- fragile setup,
- weak tests,
- migration chaos,
- poor release discipline,
- missing telemetry,
- weak secret handling,
- or bad team workflow.

## Where AI helps most

AI is useful for:
- repo analysis,
- summarization,
- documentation drafts,
- architecture mapping,
- test idea generation,
- small refactor proposals,
- standards and checklist creation,
- and backlog shaping.

## Where AI needs caution

AI can easily:
- overstate confidence,
- hallucinate architecture,
- generate shallow tests,
- suggest unsafe refactors,
- or produce more code than the team can validate.

## Final takeaway

Good engineering fundamentals do not compete with AI. They make AI more powerful.
